How the Endocrine System Works
Endocrine glands
Ductless glands that secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream (endo- = within + -crine = to secrete)
Hormones
Chemical messengers carried in blood to target organs; regulate metabolism, growth, reproduction, and homeostasis
Negative feedback
Primary control mechanism: rising hormone level signals gland to STOP secreting; falling level signals to START again
Exocrine vs. endocrine
Exocrine (exo- = outside): secretions released through ducts to surfaces (sweat glands, salivary glands). Endocrine: no ducts, into blood
Target organs
Specific cells/organs with receptors for a particular hormone; only target cells respond
Hypothalamus-pituitary axis
Hypothalamus (thalam/o) controls pituitary gland; pituitary controls most other endocrine glands — "master gland"
Specialty: Endocrinology (endo- + crin/o + -logy) · Specialist: Endocrinologist  |  Key cf: crin/o (to secrete), aden/o (gland), thyr/o (thyroid), adren/o (adrenal)
Endocrine Glands at a Glance
Hypothalamus (thalam/o) Pituitary (hypophys/o) Thyroid (thyr/o, thyroid/o) Parathyroid Adrenal (adren/o) Pancreas — islets (pancreat/o) Thymus (thym/o) Gonads (gonad/o) Pineal gland
Adenohypophysis (aden/o + hypophys/o) = anterior pituitary (glandular tissue). Neurohypophysis = posterior pituitary (neural tissue).
Key Electrolyte Terms
Sodium (natr/o) — hyponatremia = low blood sodium
Potassium (kal/i) — hypokalemia = low blood potassium
Calcium (calc/o) — hypercalcemia = high blood calcium; hypocalcemia = low
Glucose (gluc/o, glyc/o) — hypoglycemia = low blood sugar; hyperglycemia = high
The prefix hypo- = below/low and hyper- = above/high. These pair with electrolyte or hormone combining forms + -emia (blood condition).
Endocrine Glands — Location, Hormones & Functions
Pituitary Gland (hypophys/o)
Sella turcica of sphenoid bone; "master gland"
Anterior (adenohypophysis): GH (growth), TSH (thyroid), ACTH (adrenal cortex), FSH & LH (gonads), prolactin (milk)
Posterior (neurohypophysis): ADH/vasopressin (water reabsorption), Oxytocin (uterine contractions, milk ejection)
Thyroid Gland (thyr/o, thyroid/o)
Anterior neck, over trachea; butterfly-shaped
T3 (triiodothyronine) & T4 (thyroxine): regulate metabolism, growth, body temperature, heart rate
Calcitonin: lowers blood calcium (inhibits osteoclasts, promotes bone deposition)
Parathyroid Glands
4 tiny glands embedded in posterior thyroid
PTH (parathyroid hormone): RAISES blood calcium — stimulates osteoclasts (bone resorption), increases Ca reabsorption in kidneys, activates Vitamin D
Adrenal Glands (adren/o)
Superior poles of each kidney; two functional zones
Cortex: Cortisol (glucocorticoid — stress, metabolism), Aldosterone (mineralocorticoid — Na/K balance), Androgens
Medulla: Epinephrine & Norepinephrine (fight-or-flight; sympathetic response)
Pancreas — Islets of Langerhans (pancreat/o)
Retroperitoneal; dual exocrine and endocrine function
Beta cells → Insulin: LOWERS blood glucose; promotes glucose uptake by cells
Alpha cells → Glucagon: RAISES blood glucose; promotes glycogenolysis in liver
Exocrine: digestive enzymes (amylase, lipase, protease) → duodenum
Thymus Gland (thym/o)
Mediastinum, anterior to heart; largest in children; involutes with age
Thymosin: stimulates T-lymphocyte maturation and differentiation; critical for immune system development
Thymoma = tumor of thymus; associated with myasthenia gravis
Calcitonin vs. PTH: Both regulate calcium but in OPPOSITE directions. Calcitonin (thyroid) LOWERS calcium. PTH (parathyroid) RAISES calcium. "Calcitonin tones down calcium."
The Hypothalamus-Pituitary Axis — "Command Center"
The hypothalamus (thalam/o) integrates nervous and endocrine systems. It produces releasing hormones → stimulate anterior pituitary → produce tropic hormones (-trop/o) → stimulate target glands.
Anterior Pituitary Hormones (adenohypophysis)
GH — Growth hormone — stimulates growth of bones/muscles; deficiency (GHD) in children → dwarfism; excess → acromegaly (adults)
TSH — Thyroid-stimulating hormone — stimulates thyroid to produce T3/T4
ACTH — Adrenocorticotropic hormone — stimulates adrenal cortex → cortisol; excess → Cushing disease
FSH & LH — Gonadotropins (gonad/o + trop/o) — stimulate gonads (ovaries/testes)
Prolactin — stimulates milk production (lactation)
Posterior Pituitary Hormones (neurohypophysis)
ADH / Vasopressin
Anti-diuretic hormone; promotes water reabsorption in collecting ducts of kidneys; deficiency → diabetes insipidus (DI) → massive dilute urine output
Oxytocin
Stimulates uterine contractions during labor (parturition) and milk ejection (let-down reflex) during breastfeeding; also promotes bonding
-trop/o suffix = turning toward / stimulating. Tropic hormones stimulate target glands: thyrotropin (TSH), corticotropin (ACTH), gonadotropin (FSH/LH).
Insulin vs. Glucagon
Insulin (Beta cells)
Released when blood glucose is HIGH. LOWERS glucose by: promoting cellular uptake (especially muscle/fat), glycogen synthesis (glycogenesis), and inhibiting gluconeogenesis. Deficiency → diabetes mellitus.
Glucagon (Alpha cells)
Released when blood glucose is LOW. RAISES glucose by: stimulating glycogenolysis (glycogen → glucose) and gluconeogenesis in liver. Antagonist to insulin.
Memory: Insulin "invites" glucose INTO cells. Glucagon "goes to get" more glucose from liver.
Adrenal Cortex Hormones — SAG
Sex hormones (Androgens)
DHEA; minor sex hormones from zona reticularis
Aldosterone (Mineralocorticoid)
Zona glomerulosa; regulates Na⁺ retention and K⁺ excretion → controls blood pressure and fluid balance; kal/i = potassium
Glucocorticoids (Cortisol)
Zona fasciculata; stress response; anti-inflammatory; raises blood glucose; excess → Cushing syndrome
SAG mnemonic (from superficial to deep): Salt (aldosterone) · Sugar (cortisol) · Sex (androgens)
Acromegaly
Excess GH in ADULTS after growth plates fuse → overgrowth of hands, feet, jaw, facial features; organomegaly. acr/o (extremities) + -megaly (enlargement). Caused by pituitary adenoma. Treatment: surgery, radiation, somatostatin analogs.
Growth hormone deficiency (GHD)
Insufficient GH in children → proportional short stature (pituitary dwarfism); in adults → decreased muscle/bone mass, fatigue. Treatment: recombinant GH injections.
Diabetes insipidus (DI)
ADH (vasopressin) deficiency (central) or kidney resistance (nephrogenic) → inability to concentrate urine → massive polyuria (20+ L/day) and polydipsia. Blood glucose NORMAL (vs. DM). Treatment: desmopressin (synthetic ADH).
Cushing disease vs. Cushing syndrome
Cushing disease: excess ACTH from pituitary adenoma → cortisol excess. Cushing syndrome: any cause of cortisol excess (most common: long-term corticosteroid use). Features: moon face, buffalo hump, central obesity, striae, hypertension, hyperglycemia, hypokalemia.
Hypothyroidism
Insufficient thyroid hormone (T3/T4). hypo- + thyr/o. Most common: Hashimoto thyroiditis (autoimmune). Symptoms: weight gain, fatigue, cold intolerance, bradycardia, constipation, myxedema (severe). Labs: ↑TSH, ↓T4. Treatment: levothyroxine (synthetic T4).
Hyperthyroidism
Excess thyroid hormone. hyper- + thyr/o. Most common: Graves disease (autoimmune — TSH receptor antibodies). Symptoms: weight loss, heat intolerance, tachycardia, palpitations, anxiety, exophthalmia (exo- + ophthalm/o = eye protrusion — Graves). Labs: ↓TSH, ↑T4. Treatment: antithyroids, radioactive iodine, thyroidectomy.
Goiter
Enlarged thyroid gland; can occur in hypothyroidism, hyperthyroidism, or iodine deficiency. Visible neck swelling.
Thyroid carcinoma
Most common: papillary (excellent prognosis). Detected by ultrasound and fine-needle aspiration. Treatment: thyroidectomy ± radioactive iodine. TFTs (thyroid function tests): TSH, free T4 — key diagnostic labs.
Hyperparathyroidism
Excess PTH → hypercalcemia (calc/o + hyper- + -emia). "Bones, stones, groans, moans": bone pain, kidney stones, GI symptoms, psychiatric symptoms. Primary (parathyroid adenoma) or secondary (renal failure).
Hypoparathyroidism
Insufficient PTH → hypocalcemia → muscle cramps, tetany, paresthesia, seizures. Often post-thyroidectomy (inadvertent parathyroid removal). Treatment: calcium + vitamin D supplements.
Cushing syndrome (cortisol excess)
Hypercortisolism; any source. Features: central obesity, moon face, buffalo hump, purple abdominal striae, thin skin, hypertension, hyperglycemia, hypokalemia. Diagnosis: 24-hr urinary cortisol, low-dose dexamethasone suppression test.
Addison disease (adrenal insufficiency)
Destruction of adrenal cortex → deficiency of cortisol AND aldosterone. Autoimmune (most common in developed world). Features: hyperpigmentation (bronze skin), hypotension, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, weakness, weight loss. Treatment: hydrocortisone + fludrocortisone.
Pheochromocytoma
Tumor of adrenal medulla → excess epinephrine/norepinephrine → episodic hypertensive crises, palpitations, headache, diaphoresis ("5 P's": Pressure, Palpitations, Perspiration, Pain headache, Pallor). Diagnosis: urine/plasma catecholamines, metanephrines. Treatment: surgical adrenalectomy.
Conn syndrome (Primary hyperaldosteronism)
Excess aldosterone from adrenal adenoma → sodium retention, hypertension, hypokalemia (kal/i + hypo- + -emia). Diagnosis: aldosterone:renin ratio. Treatment: adrenalectomy or aldosterone antagonists (spironolactone).
The 3 Polys of Diabetes Mellitus: Polyuria (poly- + -uria: excessive urination) · Polydipsia (poly- + -dipsia: excessive thirst) · Polyphagia (poly- + phag/o + -ia: excessive eating)
Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus
Mechanism: Autoimmune destruction of beta cells → absolute insulin deficiency
Onset: Usually childhood/adolescence; lean patients
Symptoms: 3 Polys + weight loss + DKA (diabetic ketoacidosis)
Labs: ↑↑glucose, ↑A1c, glucosuria, ketonuria, no insulin
Treatment: Insulin therapy (always required)
Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus
Mechanism: Insulin resistance + relative insulin deficiency; beta cells fail over time
Onset: Usually adults; overweight; strong genetic component
Symptoms: Often asymptomatic early; 3 Polys when symptomatic
Labs: ↑glucose, ↑A1c, glucosuria; HHS (hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state) in crisis
Treatment: Lifestyle, metformin, other oral agents, then insulin if needed
Diabetes Monitoring & Labs
A1c (HbA1c) — glycosylated hemoglobin; reflects average blood glucose over 2–3 months; normal <5.7%, prediabetes 5.7–6.4%, diabetes ≥6.5%
FPG (fasting plasma glucose) — fasting blood glucose; diabetes ≥126 mg/dL
Glucometer — gluc/o + -meter; device for self-monitoring blood glucose
Glucosuria — gluc/o + -uria; glucose in urine; renal threshold exceeded (~180 mg/dL)
Ketonuria — ketones in urine; indicates fat breakdown (DKA in Type 1)
Hyponatremia (natr/o)
Low blood sodium (<135 mEq/L). hypo- + natr/o + -emia. Causes: SIADH (excess ADH), heart failure, cirrhosis, excessive water intake. Symptoms: headache, confusion, seizures (if severe). natr/o = sodium.
Hypokalemia (kal/i)
Low blood potassium (<3.5 mEq/L). hypo- + kal/i + -emia. Causes: diuretics, vomiting, Cushing/Conn syndrome, alkalosis. Symptoms: muscle weakness, cramps, arrhythmias. kal/i = potassium.
Hypercalcemia (calc/o)
High blood calcium (>10.5 mg/dL). hyper- + calc/o + -emia. Causes: hyperparathyroidism (#1), malignancy. "Bones, stones, groans, moans" — bone pain, kidney stones, GI symptoms, psychiatric symptoms. calc/o = calcium.
Hypoglycemia (gluc/o)
Low blood glucose (<70 mg/dL). hypo- + gluc/o + -emia. Symptoms: shakiness, diaphoresis, tachycardia, confusion, loss of consciousness. Causes: insulin overdose, missed meal, alcohol. Treatment: "rule of 15" — 15g fast carbs.
Anorexia / Polyphagia
Anorexia (an- + orex/o + -ia): loss of appetite; anorexia nervosa = eating disorder with self-starvation. Polyphagia (poly- + phag/o + -ia): excessive eating/hunger; classic DM symptom when cells can't use glucose.
Thymoma
Tumor of the thymus gland. thym/o + -oma. May be benign or malignant. Associated with myasthenia gravis (autoimmune neuromuscular disorder). Treated with thymectomy.
Diagnostic Procedures & Lab Tests
A1c (HbA1c)
Glycosylated hemoglobin; 2–3 month glucose average; gold standard for DM monitoring; ≥6.5% = diabetes diagnosis
Fasting plasma glucose (FPG)
Blood glucose after 8-hr fast; ≥126 mg/dL = diabetes; 100–125 = prediabetes; normal <100 mg/dL
Glucometer (gluc/o + -meter)
Handheld device for self-monitoring blood glucose; uses fingerstick blood drop on test strip
Thyroid function tests (TFTs)
TSH (most sensitive first-line), free T4, free T3; TSH ↑ = hypothyroidism; TSH ↓ = hyperthyroidism
Serum calcium / PTH levels
Diagnoses hyperparathyroidism (↑PTH + ↑Ca) vs. hypoparathyroidism (↓PTH + ↓Ca)
24-hr urinary cortisol
Screens for Cushing syndrome; elevated in hypercortisolism
Urinalysis (glucosuria, ketonuria)
Detects glucose and ketones in urine; indicates poorly controlled DM (glucosuria) or DKA (ketonuria)
MRI/CT pituitary
Identifies pituitary adenomas; used in diagnosis of acromegaly, Cushing disease, GHD
Therapeutic Procedures
Thyroid
Thyroidectomy (thyr/o + -ectomy)
Surgical removal of thyroid gland (total or partial); treats thyroid cancer, hyperthyroidism, large goiter; risk: hypoparathyroidism, laryngeal nerve injury
Radioactive iodine (RAI) therapy
I-131 taken orally; absorbed by thyroid → destroys thyroid tissue; treats hyperthyroidism and thyroid cancer
Pancreas
Pancreatectomy (pancreat/o + -ectomy)
Surgical removal of pancreas (total or partial); for pancreatic cancer, chronic pancreatitis; total → requires insulin and enzyme replacement
Pharmacology
Insulin (DM Type 1) Metformin (DM Type 2) Levothyroxine (hypothyroidism) Antithyroids — PTU, methimazole Desmopressin (DI) Hydrocortisone (Addison) Calcium + Vit D (hypoparathyroidism) Corticosteroids (various)
Lob/o in endocrine context: Refers to lobes of glands. The thyroid has right and left lobes (lob/o). The pituitary has anterior and posterior lobes. Lobectomy = removal of a lobe.
